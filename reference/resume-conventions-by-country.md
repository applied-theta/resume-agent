# Resume Conventions by Country and Region

Static reference for regional differences in resume formatting, content expectations, and cultural norms. Agents should consult this when the candidate's target market is known or when advising on international applications.

> **Note:** Conventions within a country can vary by industry, seniority level, and individual company culture. The guidance below represents mainstream expectations. When in doubt, prioritize the specific employer's stated preferences over general regional norms.

---

## United States

### Terminology

- The document is called a **resume** (not a CV, except in academia and medicine)
- A **CV** in the US context refers to a longer academic document listing publications, grants, and teaching experience
- "Cover letter" is the standard companion document

### Sections (typical order)

1. **Contact Information** -- name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL, city/state (full address is uncommon)
2. **Summary or Professional Summary** -- 2-4 sentence overview (optional but common for experienced candidates)
3. **Experience** -- reverse chronological, with company name, title, dates, and bullet points
4. **Education** -- degree, institution, graduation year; GPA included only for recent graduates (above 3.5)
5. **Skills** -- technical skills, tools, certifications
6. **Optional sections** -- Projects, Certifications, Volunteer Work, Publications

### Photo Policy

- **Do not include a photo.** This is a strong norm in the US. Photos introduce bias risk, and many employers actively discard resumes with photos to avoid discrimination liability.

### Length Expectations

| Experience Level | Recommended Length |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Entry level (0-5 years) | 1 page |
| Mid-career (5-15 years) | 1-2 pages |
| Senior/executive (15+ years) | 2 pages (rarely 3) |
| Academic CV | No limit (commonly 3-10+ pages) |

### Date Format

- **Month YYYY** (e.g., "January 2024" or "Jan 2024")
- Ranges written as "January 2022 -- Present" or "Jan 2022 - Dec 2024"

### Cultural Notes

- Results-oriented: bullet points should quantify impact ("increased revenue by 25%," "reduced latency by 40ms")
- Action verbs at the start of each bullet ("Led," "Designed," "Implemented")
- Personal pronouns are omitted ("Led team of 5" not "I led a team of 5")
- Age, marital status, nationality, religion, and gender are never included
- "References available upon request" is considered outdated and unnecessary
- Objective statements have largely been replaced by professional summaries

## United Kingdom

### Terminology

- The document is called a **CV** (curriculum vitae), not a resume
- A **covering letter** is the standard companion document (not "cover letter," though both are understood)

### Sections (typical order)

1. **Personal Details** -- name, phone, email, LinkedIn; city is common, full address is declining
2. **Personal Statement / Profile** -- brief professional overview (2-4 sentences)
3. **Work Experience** -- reverse chronological with company, title, dates, and achievements
4. **Education** -- institution, degree, dates; A-levels and GCSEs included for early-career candidates
5. **Skills** -- technical and professional skills
6. **Interests** (optional) -- more common than in the US, especially for early-career candidates
7. **References** -- historically included directly or as "Available on request," though this is declining

### Photo Policy

- **Do not include a photo.** Similar to the US, photos are discouraged to avoid discrimination. The Equality Act 2010 reinforces this norm.

### Length Expectations

| Experience Level | Recommended Length |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Entry level / graduate | 1-2 pages |
| Experienced professional | 2 pages |
| Senior / executive | 2-3 pages |
| Academic CV | No limit |

### Date Format

- **Month YYYY** (e.g., "January 2024")
- UK date format (DD/MM/YYYY) may appear but Month YYYY is cleaner for CVs

### Cultural Notes

- Slightly more narrative tone is acceptable compared to US resumes
- "Personal statement" or "profile" is used instead of "summary"
- Education section may carry more weight than in the US, especially for Oxbridge or Russell Group universities
- Hobbies/interests section is more socially acceptable and may be expected for junior roles
- National Insurance number should never be included
- Right-to-work status may be relevant to mention if applicable

## European Union (Continental Europe)

> **Important:** Europe is not monolithic. Conventions vary significantly between countries. This section covers general patterns with specific country notes where they diverge.

### Terminology

- **CV** is the standard term across most of Europe
- **Lebenslauf** (Germany/Austria), **curriculum vitae** (Italy, Spain), **resum** (France)

### Sections (general European order)

1. **Personal Information** -- name, phone, email, date of birth (common in many EU countries), nationality (sometimes)
2. **Professional Profile / Summary** -- brief overview
3. **Work Experience** -- reverse chronological
4. **Education** -- reverse chronological, often including secondary education
5. **Languages** -- proficiency levels, often using the CEFR framework (A1-C2)
6. **Skills** -- technical and professional
7. **Additional** -- publications, volunteer work, driver's license (relevant in some countries)

### Photo Policy

> **This is where regional variation is most significant.**

| Country/Region | Photo Expectation |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Germany, Austria, Switzerland | **Expected.** Professional headshot is standard, though declining among younger applicants and in tech |
| France | **Common.** Professional photo is traditional, though not legally required |
| Netherlands, Belgium | **Optional.** Neither expected nor unusual |
| Scandinavia (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland) | **Not expected.** Similar to US/UK norms; photos are uncommon |
| Spain, Italy, Portugal | **Common.** Professional photo is traditional |
| Eastern Europe (Poland, Czech Republic, etc.) | **Often expected.** Traditional CVs include photos |

### Length Expectations

- **1-2 pages** is standard across most of Europe
- Germany tends toward longer, more detailed CVs (2-3 pages are acceptable)
- Scandinavia prefers concise 1-2 page CVs

### Date Format

- **MM/YYYY** or **Month YYYY** are both common
- Country-specific date ordering (DD.MM.YYYY in Germany, DD/MM/YYYY in France) may appear

### Country-Specific Notes

#### Germany / Austria / Switzerland (DACH Region)

- Date of birth is traditionally included (declining in tech sector)
- Marital status was historically included but is becoming optional
- Education section is very detailed, including Abitur grades
- A **Europass CV** format is accepted but considered generic; a tailored CV is preferred
- Chronological order (not reverse) was traditional but reverse chronological is now more common
- Handwritten cover letters were once expected in Germany; this practice has largely ended

#### France

- Date of birth is commonly included
- The term **lettre de motivation** is used for the cover letter
- Education (especially grandes ecoles) carries significant weight
- Language proficiency is important and should be explicitly stated with levels

#### Scandinavia

- Personal identification numbers (personnummer in Sweden, etc.) should never be included
- Egalitarian culture means less emphasis on prestigious titles or institutions
- Clean, minimal formatting is preferred
- Volunteer work and personal interests are viewed positively

#### Netherlands

- **Date of birth** is commonly included but declining
- Driver's license status is commonly mentioned
- Direct, factual tone is preferred over self-promotional language

### Europass Format

The EU provides a standardized CV template called **Europass** (europass.cedefop.europa.eu). It is recognized across EU member states and useful for cross-border applications, but many employers and recruiters consider it generic and prefer tailored CVs. It is most useful for:
- Applications to EU institutions
- Cross-border job applications where format familiarity matters
- Early-career candidates without a preferred format

## Australia

### Terminology

- Both **resume** and **CV** are used; "resume" is more common for corporate roles, "CV" for academic and government positions
- **Cover letter** is the standard companion document

### Sections (typical order)

1. **Contact Details** -- name, phone, email, LinkedIn, city/state
2. **Professional Summary / Career Objective** -- brief overview
3. **Key Skills** -- often presented early, before experience
4. **Work Experience** -- reverse chronological with achievements
5. **Education** -- qualifications, institution, dates
6. **Certifications / Professional Development** -- industry certifications, courses
7. **Referees** -- historically, 2-3 referees were listed directly on the resume; this is declining but still more common than in the US/UK

### Photo Policy

- **Do not include a photo.** Anti-discrimination legislation (Fair Work Act, state equal opportunity laws) discourages photos. This is consistent with US/UK norms.

### Length Expectations

| Experience Level | Recommended Length |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Entry level / graduate | 1-2 pages |
| Mid-career | 2-3 pages |
| Senior / executive | 3-4 pages |
| Government / public sector | 3-5 pages (more detailed) |

> Australian resumes tend to be longer than US resumes. 3-4 pages is not unusual for experienced professionals, and government applications may expect even more detail.

### Date Format

- **Month YYYY** (e.g., "January 2024")
- Australian date format is DD/MM/YYYY but written date format is preferred on resumes

### Cultural Notes

- Australian resumes tend to be more detailed than US resumes; thoroughness is valued over brevity
- Government and public sector applications often require addressing **selection criteria** -- structured responses to specific job requirements, sometimes as a separate document
- Including referees (name, title, phone, email) directly on the resume is still common, though "Available on request" is acceptable
- Visa/work rights status is relevant to mention if the candidate is not a citizen or permanent resident
- Informal tone is slightly more acceptable than in the US/UK, reflecting Australian communication culture
- "Key achievements" sections within each role are well-received
- Professional memberships and industry affiliations carry weight

## Canada

### Terminology

- **Resume** is standard for most industries
- **CV** is used in academia and medicine, similar to US convention
- **Cover letter** is the standard companion document

### Sections (typical order)

1. **Contact Information** -- name, phone, email, LinkedIn, city/province
2. **Professional Summary** -- brief overview
3. **Experience** -- reverse chronological
4. **Education** -- degree, institution, dates
5. **Skills** -- technical and professional
6. **Optional** -- Certifications, Volunteer Work, Languages

### Photo Policy

- **Do not include a photo.** Canadian human rights legislation discourages photos for the same reasons as the US.

### Length Expectations

| Experience Level | Recommended Length |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Entry level | 1 page |
| Experienced | 1-2 pages |
| Senior / executive | 2-3 pages |
| Federal government | May be longer, following specific application formats |

### Cultural Notes

- Very similar to US conventions in structure and tone
- **Bilingual resumes** (English/French) may be relevant for positions in Quebec or federal government
- Federal government applications use a distinct format and may require specific information
- Language proficiency (especially French) should be explicitly stated when relevant
- Volunteer experience is valued and commonly included
- Canadian spelling conventions (colour, programme, etc.) should be used when applying to Canadian employers

## Additional Regions (Brief Notes)

### Japan

- Uses a standardized resume format called **rirekisho** (handwritten or typed on a prescribed template)
- Photo is required (professional, passport-style)
- Date of birth, gender, and marital status are included
- Reverse chronological is standard
- A separate **shokumu keirekisho** (career history document) provides detailed work experience

### Middle East (UAE, Saudi Arabia, etc.)

- Photo is commonly expected
- Nationality, date of birth, visa status, and sometimes religion may be included
- Longer CVs (2-4 pages) are common
- International candidates should note visa/work permit status

### India

- **Resume** and **CV** are both used
- Photo is common but not universal
- Personal details (date of birth, father's name, marital status) are traditionally included but declining in modern tech companies
- **Declaration** section at the end ("I hereby declare that the above information is true...") is traditional but declining

### China

- Photo is expected
- Personal details include date of birth, gender, marital status, and political affiliation (sometimes)
- Hukou (household registration) status may be relevant
- Education section is prominently positioned

> **When uncertain about a specific country's conventions**, research the target country's norms or advise the candidate to follow the conventions of the country where the job is located, not their home country.
